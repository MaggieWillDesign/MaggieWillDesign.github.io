Justfab

An e-commerce brand offering customers modern, on-trend fashion at affordable prices, with an extensive product assortment.

Project Overview


The Problem

JustFab’s growth had reached a plateau, as a KPI-centric approach created a fragmented user experience, highlighting the need for a cohesive brand revamp and a more user-centered acquisition strategy.

On the growth team, I was exposed to a range of acquisition metrics and KPI terminology—CPCs, CPAs, CACs, CPLs, ROAS, AOV, CPMs, LTV, and more. While it was thrilling to work with such data and constantly strive to surpass previous benchmarks, I often wondered if this intense KPI focus was diluting the overall user experience. Our growth strategy also showed clear signs of inefficiency, with a lack of design cohesion, bottlenecks, and outdated processes. At first, these concerns seemed secondary, as the business was thriving. However, when growth stalled, the need for fresh, user-centered perspectives became evident.

To commemorate JustFab’s 10th anniversary, the company planned an extensive brand revamp. This involved establishing a new brand identity, including updated fonts, styling, and foundational enhancements to the e-commerce site. Cross-functional collaboration—spanning marketing, creative, merchandising, product, and engineering—presented the ideal moment to also reimagine our prospecting growth experiences, aligning them with a stronger, more unified brand identity.

The Goal

With a rebrand on the horizon, the objective was to conduct a UX audit of acquisition funnels and develop strategies to resolve design inconsistencies and outdated processes. The focus was on designing and implementing proven, growth-driven solutions to optimize new user conversion rates, and retention through comprehensive research, data analysis, and A/B testing. The goal is to create user-centric designs that address behaviors, needs, and growth challenges while enhancing usability across all customer prospecting and lead touch points.

My Role: Product Design, User Research, UX Design, Visual Design

Responsibilities: Conduct user research, define the problem, and provide insights to inform ideation. Define personas, user journeys, empathy maps, and user flows. Visual design of low-fi and high-fi wireframes, prototypes, and user testing. Design system development.

Timeframe: Justfab Q3-Q4 2022

User Research Goals

  • Understand Common Pain Points

  • Gauge Decision-Making Factors

  • Measure User Satisfaction and Engagement

  • Analyze Behavioral Patterns

User Research Summary

To begin this project, we set out to gather both quantitative and qualitative insights from users, focusing on the experiences of first-time and returning visitors who had not yet converted into leads or completed a purchase. We then conducted an in-depth analysis of existing data and behavioral analytics, using heatmaps and session recordings to identify areas of engagement and points of drop-off in the customer journey. Additionally, we closely examined NPS data and customer attrition metrics to gain a fuller understanding of user satisfaction and retention.

User Pain Points

1. Style Quiz Struggle

Many users mentioned finding it difficult to shop the site, mainly due to a style quiz and lead gen form that is forced on them when all they want to do is look at a product and add to cart and checkout. We also had a best sellers scroller that our heatmaps indicated was the top clicked element on all our pages. However this section was populated with hard coded product images and clicked out to the quiz intsead of a product page as users would expect.

2. Product Discoverability

A overwhelmingly common frustration was that users were unable to find the products they saw in Ads. Customers were confused after being excited about a product and ready to purchase, only to arrive at the site unsure how to find the specific item.

3. Lack of Trust Signals

Users noticed a disconnect in the logged out vs logged in experience during their customer journey. First-time users also expressed concern about trusting the brand due to unfamiliarity, with some stating that they needed more information, social proof or brand awareness before feeling confident enough to make a purchase.

4. Complicated Checkout Flow

Users mentioned that they found the checkout process confusing with the membership language and with too many steps or legal disclaimers. Some suggested simplifying the payment and shipping information fields.

Accessibility Considerations

1.

We reviewed the accessible color combinations, listed in the chart below. Following ADA compliance, the chart shows available background and text colors with contrast ratios meeting WCAG 2 level AA: at least 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text, and 3:1 for UI components. These combinations are ready to apply on the site.

2.

Two typefaces are utilized Savoy serif for headlines and Gibson san-serif for body copy. A limited set of typefaces prevents the experience from appearing fragmented and enhances user comprehension by providing clear focal points.

3.

Different font weights and colors are used for visual hierarchy across the website, aiding users in discerning various sections and information displayed on the screen.

High-fidelity Prototypes

After completing the low-fidelity prototypes and establishing the design system, we refined the final prototypes using the established components. We collaborated closely with our merchandising and creative teams to incorporate strategic photography choices that would convert new customers and align with the brand's new visual language. The prototypes also showcase how the refined experience is now cohesive for each customer segment.

Product Discoverability

This showcases a dynamic functionality that allows users to seamlessly shop products featured in ads while also learning about the membership and other relevant products. The logic, outlined in the user flow, operates through a unique parameter that is dynamically appended to the URL when a user taps or clicks on a paid ad. No additional effort is required, as ads are linked to a feed aggregator, automating the process of passing the product parameter.

Takeaways

Impact

With this project, I have gained valuable insights into the diverse approaches users have towards fitness and the recurring challenges they often face. While the app aims to cater to individuals of all fitness levels, there is significant room for further improvement and accessibility solutions. The initial goal was to initiate the process of providing more customization features, moving away from a one-size-fits-all approach.

What I learned

Throughout the design journey, I've gained valuable insights and expertise. Key lessons learned encompass:

  • Understanding user needs

  • Emphasizing the significance of hierarchy

  • Considering accessibility considerations

  • Effectively analyzing and synthesizing user feedback
